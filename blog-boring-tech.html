The Beauty of Boring Tech.

We work in an industry obsessed with the new. New frameworks, new paradigms, new languages. It's incredibly exciting, but when you are trying to ship a real product to real users, this excitement can quickly become a liability.

I've recently found myself gravitating towards boring technology. Postgres. Redis. Plain CSS. Vanilla JavaScript.

Predictability over Hype

Boring tech doesn't mean bad tech. It means predictable tech. It means that when something breaks at 2 AM, the solution is already thoroughly documented on Stack Overflow by someone who encountered the exact same issue five years ago.

When you choose a new, shiny tool, you're not just adopting its features; you're adopting its unknown bugs, its lack of an ecosystem, and its potential to be abandoned by its maintainers in a year.

Focus on the Product

The user doesn't care if your backend is written in Rust or if you're using the latest edge-compute serverless functions. They care if the app is fast, if it works, and if it solves their problem.

By using boring tech, you spend less time fighting your tools and more time building features that actually matter.
